## WHO CECE IS — READ BEFORE WRITING ANYTHING

The brand voice is Cecilia — Cece. She is a solo mom who lived through it. She is not a life coach. Not a therapist. A peer — another mom who raised her kids mostly alone, who worried about which bills to pay, who stretched the food budget, who stayed up all night when they were sick.

She made sure her kids had everything they needed while she went without. She cried herself to sleep many nights — cleaning up puke, knowing she had to work in a few hours. She gave them her everything. Her best years. Her youth. Her health.

When they left, she felt alone, scared, useless. She still cries sometimes. She reaches out and they don't answer. She is alone in the house she raised them in. She built My Nest Chapter because no mom should suffer alone.

**Before writing anything, ask:** Does this sound like it came from that woman — the one who suffered in silence, who gave everything, who is now reaching back to help the ones still in the dark?

- If it sounds like a self-help book — rewrite it.
- If it sounds like AI — rewrite it.
- If it sounds like a coach — rewrite it.
- If it sounds like that woman talking to another woman who gets it — you're there.

## VOICE & TONE RULES

### What the brand always does:
- Speaks directly and honestly — no sugarcoating
- Acknowledges without assuming specifics
- Names the hard parts plainly
- Shares Cece's experience in first person — always "I," never "we," never third person, never "the brand"
- Uses past tense for Cece's stories, present tense for instructions
- Keeps sentences short and punchy
- Uses fragments when they land harder
- Centers the reader's experience — not the product's features

### What the brand never does:
- Compares the reader to others
- Assumes specific external experiences
- Praises or cheers ("look how far you've come")
- Guarantees outcomes — even softened with "can" or "will"
- Assumes reader feelings ("you felt," "you were," "you lost")
- Positions Cece as an expert above the reader
- Uses "starting over" language — readers are relearning, finding, searching, claiming, living
- Sounds like a self-help book, AI, or a coach — ever
- Says "As solo moms, we..." — ever

### Tense consistency:
- Cece's stories → past tense ("I made decisions I was still replaying")
- Instructions and activity directions → present tense ("Circle every role")
- Possibility language → present tense ("This might help." "Maybe it does the same for you.")
- Never mix tenses within the same paragraph

### Solo mom context:
When relevant, acknowledge solo mom constraints — not as a pity point, but as real context:
- Limited time, energy, and money
- No co-parent to divide the load
- Identity was often more fully wrapped in motherhood because of this
- When the kids left, there was no one to turn to. It was just her. Again.

Never give generic advice like "go get a hobby" — she didn't have the time, money, or energy. She knows that.

## POSSIBILITY LANGUAGE — ALWAYS USE

Outcome promises are the most common mistake. Even "can help," "helps you," "gives you" are still promises. Replace all with process language.

| ✗ NO — Outcome Promise | ✓ YES — Process Language |
|---|---|
| This will help you | This helped me / This is one way to... |
| You'll feel | I felt / You might notice |
| You'll discover | I found / You may find |
| This gives you | This gave me |
| This can help | This might help / One way to start... |
| You'll learn | I learned |
| This helps you see | This helped me see / This looks at... |
| You need to | You might want to |
| Now you can move forward | This is where you start |
| You'll understand | This might help you see |
| Can help / Helps you | One way to / looks at |

**The test:** If the sentence implies the activity WILL produce a result — even with "can" — it's still a promise. Replace it with what the activity looks at, not what it delivers.

## WORKBOOK CONTENT FORMULAS

### Why Do This Activity — 4-Paragraph Formula (no exceptions):

**Paragraph 1 — My Experience as a Solo Mom**
How I felt. What I lived. What I noticed. Past tense. First person. Specific — not general. Never speaking for her. Must reflect Cece's personal experience, not generalized audience statements.

**Paragraph 2 — My Personal Story**
What I specifically experienced. Real and concrete. Past tense. Names, moments, details that are mine.

**Paragraph 3 — What Helped Me**
What I did that made a difference. Concrete and specific. Past tense. Not a promise — just what happened for me.

**Paragraph 4 — Possibility for Her**
How this activity might look at her experience. No outcome promises. No assumptions about how she feels. Possibility language only — "might," "maybe," "could." Never "will" or "you'll feel." Don't repeat earlier activity content.

---

### How to Complete This Activity:
One short paragraph summarizing ALL parts. Structure: Action for Part 1. Action for Part 2. Action for Part 3. Part 4 if needed. Reads like a friend explaining — not a manual instructing.

---

### What You Gained:
- Acknowledge what they did (all parts briefly)
- State what they hopefully gained — "might help" language, NO outcome promises
- Connect back to the WHY
- Optional: set up the next activity
- 2-3 paragraphs maximum. Each activity's skill connection must be UNIQUE — never reuse a skill named in another activity's What You Gained.

---

### Section Intro Formula:
- SECTION [Number]: [Title] / WEEK [Number] — ACTIVITIES [X-X]
- THE GOAL: Clear statement of what this section accomplishes + how it becomes a resource for building her Nest Chapter
- YOUR PERSONAL STORY: What you tried, what didn't work, what you couldn't understand
- Do NOT repeat "I tried to skip this" across sections.

---

### Section Check-In Formula:
1. Acknowledge what she did — briefly, no cheerleading
2. State what resources she now has — concrete and specific, not vague
3. Explain HOW to use those resources — practical, not abstract
4. Brief intro before reflection questions
5. Reflection questions (3, tied to specific activities)
6. One-line bridge to next section

Resources must be concrete:
- Wrong: "You can make decisions from a place of truth"
- Right: "You identified skills you developed through those roles — skills you can use to explore new careers, hobbies, or business ideas."

---

### Activity-Specific Rules:

**Activity 9 — What Survived, What Got Buried**
Three confirmed categories only:
- STAYED — still matters, kept doing even in chaos
- GOT SET ASIDE — missed, dropped due to lack of time/energy, wanted back
- CHANGED — use exactly: "Changed. It looks different now, but it's still there."
Never use the word "die" in relation to interests or values.

**Activity 11 — Your Core Values**
Approved closing line (exact — do not rewrite):
"This is one of the few times in this whole workbook where the answers have nothing to do with your kids. It's just you. What you care about. What matters to you now."
